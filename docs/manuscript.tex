\begin{abstract}
Analytic nuclear gradients and nonadiabatic coupling vectors are central quantities for conical-intersection optimization and nonadiabatic molecular dynamics with multireference wave functions.
State-averaged complete-active-space self-consistent-field (SA-CASSCF) theory is robust near electronic degeneracies, but the conventional full-configuration-interaction (FCI) active-space representation limits the active spaces for which derivative quantities can be evaluated and validated.
Here we report a certified, FCI-free matrix-product-state (MPS) response implementation for analytic SA-DMRG-CASSCF gradients and nonadiabatic couplings in a PySCF/pyblock2 workflow.
The implementation combines three ingredients: an MPS-Krylov response backend that never stores the active-space response vector as a dense determinant array, an MPS-native cross-geometry overlap protocol for finite-difference NAC validation without determinant expansion, and an a posteriori response certificate based on the true residual of the original coupled response equation and root-projector leakage.
The method is validated in two complementary regimes.
Where FCI is available, public benchmark systems verify energies, density matrices, gradients, derivative couplings, root assignment, and MPS response algebra against exact active-space references.
Where FCI is impossible, internal consistency is assessed by finite differences of the same SA-DMRG-CASSCF energy surface and by determinant-free cross-geometry MPS overlaps.
The largest planned beyond-FCI benchmark is an all-trans polyene CAS(20,20) $\pi$ space with $\binom{20}{10}^{2}=3.41\times 10^{10}$ active-space determinants, for which no dense FCI vector or FCI-overlap root tracker is used.
A sweep-localized Schur-complement response route and a certified auto-response driver provide independent response solves and same-geometry warm starts; all accepted results are gated by the same true-residual certificate.
The SHARC-compatible interface is retained as an output data contract for energies, dipoles, gradients, NACs, and phases, but the present work is not claimed to make long-time CAS(20,20) surface-hopping dynamics routine.
Instead, it establishes and validates the FCI-free derivative machinery required for large-active-space nonadiabatic workflows.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Nonadiabatic molecular processes require more electronic-structure information than adiabatic energies alone.
Surface-hopping and related mixed quantum--classical methods require state gradients and state couplings to propagate nuclei through regions where the Born--Oppenheimer approximation breaks down.\cite{Tully1990,Richter2011SHARC,Mai2018SHARC}
For same-spin internal conversion, derivative coupling vectors and state-gradient differences define the first-order topology near conical intersections and are direct inputs to crossing-point optimization and dynamics.\cite{Yarkony1996,Bernardi1996,Bearpark1994,FdezGalvan2016}
SA-CASSCF wave functions are widely used in this regime because state averaging provides a balanced multiconfigurational description of near-degenerate electronic states.\cite{Roos1980CASSCF,KnowlesWerner1985MCSCF}
The bottleneck is the active-space FCI vector, whose dimension grows combinatorially with the number of active electrons and orbitals.

The density matrix renormalization group (DMRG) replaces the dense FCI vector by a matrix product state (MPS), enabling much larger active spaces when the entanglement structure is favorable.\cite{White1992DMRG,WhiteMartin1999DMRG,RommerOstlund1997MPS,ChanHeadGordon2002,ChanSharma2011,SharmaChan2012}
DMRG-CASSCF orbital optimization has therefore become a standard route to large-active-space multireference electronic structure.\cite{ZgidNooijen2008,Ghosh2008,Wouters2014DMRGReview,OlivaresAmaya2015DMRGPractice,Wouters2014CheMPS2}
Analytic DMRG derivative theory has also advanced substantially.
Freitag and co-workers introduced SA-DMRG-SCF gradients and nonadiabatic couplings using MPS Lagrange multipliers, and Iino, Shiozaki, and Yanai formulated analytic SA-DMRG-CASSCF gradients using coupled-perturbed DMRG-CASSCF equations.\cite{Freitag2019,Iino2023}
These developments provide the theoretical foundation for DMRG-based derivative calculations.

The remaining challenge is practical and numerical rather than purely formal.
For small active spaces, an implementation can be checked against exact FCI derivatives.
For the active spaces that motivate DMRG, however, dense FCI vectors, dense response vectors, and FCI-overlap root assignment are impossible.
A useful large-active-space derivative implementation therefore needs an FCI-free validation strategy, not only an FCI-scored regression suite.
It also needs response-convergence diagnostics that remain meaningful after MPS addition, compression, and gauge projection, because a small projected Krylov residual alone may not certify the original coupled response equation.
Finally, if the implementation is to be used repeatedly along molecular paths or in dynamics interfaces, root tracking and phase alignment must be formulated without relying on determinant-space references.

This work addresses those issues in an open PySCF/pyblock2 implementation.
Its main contributions are as follows.
First, we implement analytic SA-DMRG-CASSCF gradients and derivative couplings through PySCF's CASSCF derivative infrastructure while representing active-space roots and response vectors as block2 MPS objects.
Second, we introduce an FCI-free cross-geometry MPS overlap protocol for finite-difference NAC validation without determinant expansion.
Third, we add an a posteriori response certificate based on the true residual of the original coupled response equation, root-projector leakage, and reported MPS bond dimensions.
Fourth, we provide both global MPS-Krylov and sweep-localized Schur-complement response routes, together with same-geometry warm starts for repeated right-hand sides.
Fifth, we separate interface-contract tests from dynamics claims: the SHARC-compatible output path is verified, but long-time large-active-space DMRG-SHARC dynamics is not claimed here.

\section{Theory and Algorithm}
\subsection{State-Averaged CASSCF Response}
For an SA-CASSCF wave function, orbitals and active-space state parameters are optimized for a weighted state average,
\begin{equation}
E_{\mathrm{SA}}=\sum_I w_I E_I,
\qquad
\frac{\partial E_{\mathrm{SA}}}{\partial \kappa_{pq}}=0,
\qquad
\frac{\partial E_{\mathrm{SA}}}{\partial a_{\mu I}}=0.
\end{equation}
Here $\kappa_{pq}$ are orbital-rotation parameters and $a_{\mu I}$ denotes active-space parameters in either the CI or MPS representation.
Let $\bfx$ collect all orbital and active-space parameters.
For a state-specific gradient, the response multipliers $\bfz_I$ solve
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:cp}
\bfH^{\mathrm{SA}}\bfz_I=-\frac{\partial E_I}{\partial\bfx},
\qquad
\bfH^{\mathrm{SA}}=\frac{\partial^2E_{\mathrm{SA}}}{\partial\bfx\,\partial\bfx}.
\end{equation}
The stationary Lagrangian is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal L_I=E_I+\bfz_I^{\mathrm T}\frac{\partial E_{\mathrm{SA}}}{\partial\bfx},
\end{equation}
and the analytic gradient follows from the explicit nuclear derivative at stationarity,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:gradient}
\frac{dE_I}{dR_A}=\left.\frac{\partial\mathcal L_I}{\partial R_A}\right|_{\mathrm{stat.}}.
\end{equation}
The derivative coupling vector is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:nac}
\mathbf d^A_{IJ}=\left\langle\Psi_I\middle|\frac{\partial\Psi_J}{\partial R_A}\right\rangle,
\qquad I\ne J.
\end{equation}
In the implementation, Eq.~\eqref{eq:nac} is evaluated through an off-diagonal Lagrangian $\mathcal L_{IJ}$ and assembled from the same classes of orbital-response multipliers and one- and two-particle transition-density contractions used for Eq.~\eqref{eq:gradient}.
The present work implements these established response quantities rather than deriving a new derivative Lagrangian.

\subsection{MPS Active-Space Representation}
In DMRG-CASSCF, each active-space root is represented by an MPS,
\begin{equation}
|\Psi_I(M)\rangle=\sum_{\{n\}}A^{n_1}_{1,I}A^{n_2}_{2,I}\cdots A^{n_L}_{L,I}|n_1n_2\cdots n_L\rangle,
\end{equation}
where $M$ bounds the auxiliary dimensions of the MPS tensors.
The DMRG layer replaces the dense active-space eigenvector and response-vector representation; the orbital-response equations and derivative contractions retain the PySCF SA-CASSCF structure.
The global MPS-Krylov backend represents the right-hand side, trial response vectors, and Krylov basis directly as MPS objects, using MPS overlaps for orthogonalization and MPS addition/compression for linear combinations.

\subsection{FCI-Free Cross-Geometry MPS Overlaps}
\label{sec:cross-overlap}
Finite-difference validation of NACs requires wave-function overlaps between neighboring geometries.
For determinant-space CI vectors this can be done by expanding both states in a common determinant basis, but that route is impossible in the active spaces that motivate DMRG.
We therefore transport the displaced MPS into the reference active-orbital basis before computing the overlap.

For a reference geometry $\bfR_0$ and a displaced geometry $\bfR_\pm=\bfR_0\pm h\mathbf e_k$, let $C^0_{\mathrm{act}}$ and $C^\pm_{\mathrm{act}}$ be the active orbital coefficient matrices and $S^{0\pm}$ the AO cross-overlap matrix.
The active-active overlap is
\begin{equation}
X^{0\pm}=(C^0_{\mathrm{act}})^{\mathrm T}S^{0\pm}C^\pm_{\mathrm{act}}.
\end{equation}
We compute the polar factor of $X^{0\pm}=U\Sigma V^\dagger$,
\begin{equation}
Q^{0\pm}=UV^\dagger,
\end{equation}
and apply the corresponding active-orbital rotation operator $\hat U(Q^{0\pm})$ to the displaced MPS,
\begin{equation}
|\widetilde\Psi_J(\bfR_\pm)\rangle=\hat U(Q^{0\pm})|\Psi_J(\bfR_\pm)\rangle.
\end{equation}
The minimum singular value of $X^{0\pm}$ is recorded as an active-space continuity diagnostic.
For isolated states, the reference-centered finite-difference NAC component is then
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fd-nac}
d^{k,\mathrm{FD}}_{IJ}=\frac{\langle\Psi_I(\bfR_0)|\widetilde\Psi_J(\bfR_+)\rangle-\langle\Psi_I(\bfR_0)|\widetilde\Psi_J(\bfR_-)\rangle}{2h},
\end{equation}
after phase alignment.
Near degeneracies, root labels are not unique, and the same overlaps are used to align a state subspace rather than a single root.
For a target subspace $\Omega$, the overlap matrix
\begin{equation}
O_{IJ}=\langle\Psi_I(\bfR_t)|\widetilde\Psi_J(\bfR_{t+\Delta t})\rangle,
\qquad I,J\in\Omega,
\end{equation}
is polar-decomposed and used to rotate the current subspace into the previous gauge.
The singular values of $O$ are reported as root-continuity diagnostics.

\subsection{Finite-Difference Internal Consistency}
For gradients, the finite-difference check uses the same SA-DMRG-CASSCF state energies produced by the implementation,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:fd-gradient}
g^{k,\mathrm{FD}}_I(h)=\frac{E_I(\bfR_0+h\mathbf e_k)-E_I(\bfR_0-h\mathbf e_k)}{2h}.
\end{equation}
This test does not require wave-function overlaps and is therefore available in CAS(20,20) and larger active spaces.
Displaced calculations are seeded by projecting the converged reference orbitals into the displaced AO basis and are accepted only if the active-space singular-value and root-gap guards pass.
The finite-difference step is scanned to separate response error from finite-difference truncation and numerical noise.

\subsection{A Posteriori Response Certificate}
\label{sec:certificate}
MPS-Krylov and Schur routes involve MPS compression and gauge projection.
For this reason, we do not use the projected residual of a reduced Krylov or Schur problem as the sole convergence criterion.
Every reported derivative is certified by recomputing the residual of the original coupled response equation.
For the convention $\bfH^{\mathrm{SA}}\tilde\bfz+\mathbf b=0$, the true relative residual is
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:true-residual}
\rho_{\mathrm{true}}=\frac{\|\bfH^{\mathrm{SA}}\tilde\bfz+\mathbf b\|}{\|\mathbf b\|}.
\end{equation}
The active-space response component must also remain orthogonal to the optimized reference roots.
We therefore report the root-projector leakage,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:leakage}
\lambda_{\mathrm{root}}=\max_{K}\left|\langle\Psi_K|\tilde z_C\rangle\right|.
\end{equation}
For multistate response vectors the maximum is taken over all propagated state blocks.
A derivative is accepted only when the true residual and root leakage pass the stated tolerances.
The certificate also records the ground-state and response bond dimensions, number of matrix-vector products, number of MPS compressions, wall time, and memory high-water mark.

\subsection{Sweep-Localized Schur Response}
\label{sec:schur}
Partitioning the response variables into orbital rotations and active-space parameters gives
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:coupled-blocks}
\begin{pmatrix}
\bfH_{\kappa\kappa} & \bfH_{\kappa C}\\
\bfH_{C\kappa} & \bfH_{CC}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf z_\kappa\\
\mathbf z_C
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
\mathbf b_\kappa\\
\mathbf b_C
\end{pmatrix}.
\end{equation}
The active-space block $\bfH_{CC}$ is the shifted active-space Hamiltonian projected onto the complement of the reference roots.
Eliminating the active-space block yields
\begin{align}
\label{eq:schur}
\left(\bfH_{\kappa\kappa}-\bfH_{\kappa C}\bfH_{CC}^{-1}\bfH_{C\kappa}\right)\mathbf z_\kappa
&=\mathbf b_\kappa-\bfH_{\kappa C}\bfH_{CC}^{-1}\mathbf b_C,\\
\mathbf z_C&=\bfH_{CC}^{-1}\left(\mathbf b_C-\bfH_{C\kappa}\mathbf z_\kappa\right).
\end{align}
Each $\bfH_{CC}^{-1}$ application is performed as a sweep-localized MPS correction-vector solve.
The Schur route solves the same response equation as the global MPS-Krylov route and is always checked by the certificate of Eq.~\eqref{eq:true-residual}.
Its cost advantage is system-dependent; we report wall times explicitly rather than assuming asymptotic acceleration.

\section{Implementation}
The implementation is built on PySCF and pyblock2/block2.\cite{Sun2018PySCF,Sun2020PySCF,Zhai2023Block2}
The DMRG-facing layer satisfies the solver and response contracts used by PySCF's SA-CASSCF gradient, NAC, Newton-CASSCF, and Lagrangian modules.
The public code provides four response-related paths.
First, \texttt{MPSAsFCISolver} exposes DMRG roots through a PySCF-compatible active-space solver interface.
Second, \texttt{CPDMRGCASSCFResponseMPSKrylov} stores response right-hand sides and trial vectors as MPS objects and assembles gradient and NAC Lagrangians from MPS density matrices.
Third, the sweep-localized Schur solver provides an independent route to the same coupled response equation.
Fourth, the certified auto-response driver selects a response route, applies same-geometry warm starts for repeated right-hand sides, and accepts only solutions that pass the true-residual certificate.

The FCI-free overlap layer transports MPS roots between geometries without determinant expansion.
It evaluates active-orbital polar rotations, applies orbital rotations to block2 MPS roots, aligns phases or subspaces, and returns overlaps needed for root tracking and finite-difference NAC checks.
In active spaces below a determinant threshold, the same test can be scored against determinant-level overlaps.
Above that threshold, dense FCI conversion and FCI-overlap tracking are disabled by construction.

The SHARC-facing interface is a data writer and repeated-call wrapper.
It constructs a PySCF CASSCF object, installs the DMRG-backed active-space solver, evaluates requested Hamiltonian, dipole, gradient, NAC, and phase quantities, and writes the corresponding SHARC \texttt{QM.out} blocks.
The H$_2$ and ethylene examples are used only as interface-contract regressions.
They are not used as evidence that long-time large-active-space DMRG-SHARC dynamics is routine.

\section{Computational Details}
Calculations used Python 3.11.14, PySCF 2.12.1, block2 0.5.3 through pyblock2, and SHARC 3.0.
Unless stated otherwise, calculations used singlet SA(2)-CASSCF with equal weights and spin-adapted SU2 block2 DMRG.
The fixed-orbital FCI-scored benchmarks used SA-CASSCF-converged orbitals, PySCF \texttt{direct\_spin0} FCI references, and DMRG roots assigned by overlap where determinant vectors were available.
The FCI-free finite-difference benchmarks used projected displaced-geometry orbital guesses, active-subspace singular-value guards, root-gap guards for energy-ordered roots, and no warm-start or persistent MPS cache in the displaced energy calculations.

All-trans polyenes were used for the beyond-FCI ladder.
The $\pi$ active space is CAS($n,n$) for C$_n$H$_{n+2}$.
The determinant dimension for C$_{20}$H$_{22}$ CAS(20,20) is $\binom{20}{10}^{2}=3.41\times10^{10}$, so dense FCI vectors, dense determinant overlaps, and determinant-space response vectors are disabled.
Finite-difference gradients were evaluated for selected Cartesian components with $h=2\times10^{-3}$, $1\times10^{-3}$, and $5\times10^{-4}$ Bohr.
The largest active-space result tables below should be generated from the machine-readable benchmark output; cells marked pending are to be filled only after the corresponding cluster jobs complete.

The SHARC interface-contract regressions used H$_2$ and ethylene with deliberately small active spaces and three frames.
These runs verify repeated calls and output formatting only.
A longer repeated-call robustness test in a small or medium active space is reported separately if available.

\section{Results and Discussion}
\subsection{Regression Against FCI Active-Space References}
The public regression suite verifies DMRG/FCI energies, state density matrices, transition density matrices, MPS-to-FCI conversion, analytic gradients, analytic derivative couplings, response-vector solves, and SHARC output formatting.
These tests establish algebraic and sign-convention correctness in active spaces where exact FCI references are available.
They are not used by themselves to claim DMRG necessity.

The previous FCI-scored endpoint checks remain useful as regression tests.
In all small and medium active-space cases, DMRG root selection and derivative evaluation can be run without loading FCI CI vectors at runtime; FCI data are loaded only after the endpoint calculation to score the errors.
The anthracene CAS(14,14) benchmark is retained as the largest FCI-scored bridge case.
At $M=512$, the strict MPS-Krylov response gives maximum state-wise energy, gradient, and derivative-coupling errors of $6.41\times10^{-5}$ m$\Eh$, $1.57\times10^{-3}$ m$\Eh$/Bohr, and $9.11\times10^{-6}$ a.u., respectively.
This benchmark is explicitly a validation stress test, not the final demonstration of DMRG necessity.

\subsection{Root and Subspace Tracking}
Near degeneracies, individual adiabatic root labels are gauge-dependent.
We therefore diagnose continuity of the propagated state subspace rather than assuming that an energy-sorted root label is physically meaningful.
A path test along \pending{LiF stretch, butadiene torsion, or another chosen coordinate} reports the energy gap, overlap matrix, subspace singular values, root assignment, and response residuals at each point.
This test addresses whether the overlap machinery remains stable near avoided crossings or conical-intersection-like topologies.

\section{Conclusions}
We have implemented and validated a certified, FCI-free MPS response framework for analytic SA-DMRG-CASSCF gradients and nonadiabatic couplings in PySCF/pyblock2.
The implementation retains FCI-scored regression tests where determinant vectors are available, but its central validation strategy for large active spaces is internal consistency: finite differences of the same SA-DMRG-CASSCF energy surface, MPS-native cross-geometry overlaps for NACs, root/subspace continuity diagnostics, and a posteriori true-residual response certificates.
The polyene CAS(20,20) benchmark is designed to demonstrate derivative validation in an active space with $3.41\times10^{10}$ determinants, where conventional FCI-CASSCF derivative calculations and determinant-overlap root tracking are impossible.

The Schur and auto-response routes provide certified alternatives to the global MPS-Krylov solve and enable warm-start recycling for repeated right-hand sides.
Their practical benefit is quantified by explicit timing and memory breakdowns rather than assumed from asymptotic arguments.
The SHARC-compatible interface is retained as a data-delivery layer for energies, dipoles, gradients, NACs, and phases; the present work does not claim routine long-time CAS(20,20) nonadiabatic dynamics.
Instead, it establishes the FCI-free derivative and validation machinery required for such workflows and identifies the remaining response-cost bottlenecks.
